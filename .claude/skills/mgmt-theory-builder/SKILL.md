# Management Theory Builder (AMJ Standards)

This skill provides guidance for constructing theoretical frameworks and writing theory papers in management and business research. 

## Logic Flow
Based on Thatcher & Fisher (2022), a theory paper follows three core steps:

### Step 1: Identify the Theoretical Gap
A theory gap exists when:
- There is a contradictory finding in the literature
- A construct or relationship is under-explored
- Existing theory cannot explain a phenomenon
- Boundary conditions of existing theory are unclear

**Questions to ask:**
- What don't we know yet that we should know?
- What paradoxes exist in the current understanding?
- What assumptions remain untested?

### Step 2: Build the Theory

1. **Set the Theoretical Foundation**
Before arguing specific relationships, establish the overarching theoretical lens (e.g., Resource-Based View, Social Exchange Theory).
   - Briefly summarize the core tenets of the chosen theory.
   - Explicitly justify *why* this theory is the most appropriate lens for your specific phenomenon/gap.
   - Explain how your constructs map onto the theoretical framework.

2. **Define Constructs & Contextualize**
   - Provide clear, distinct definitions
   - Establish construct boundaries
   - **Crucial:** Contextualize the construct. How does this general construct manifest in your specific research setting?

3. **Deduce Main Effects / Baseline Logic**
Do not just list previous findings (that's a literature review, not theory building). Build an argument (The A -> B argument).
   - **Premise:** State the initial condition based on your theoretical lens.
   - **Process:** Explain the *action* or *cognitive/behavioral shift* that Construct A triggers.
   - **Outcome:** Explain how this shift inevitably leads to Construct B.
   - **Conclusion:** Lead naturally into the formal proposition/hypothesis.

4. **Unpack the Black Box: Mechanisms / Mediation**
AMJ places a heavy emphasis on explaining the *mechanisms* transmitting the effect (The A -> M -> B argument).
   - Argue why Construct A specifically activates the Mediator (M).
   - Argue why the Mediator (M) specifically drives Construct B.
   - Ensure the mechanism perfectly aligns with your overarching theoretical lens (Stage 1).

5. **Specify Boundary Conditions**
No theory works everywhere. You must specify when the relationship is stronger or weaker.
   - Introduce the moderator and define it.
   - Explain the *interaction logic*: How does the moderator change the environment, motivation, or capability of the actor?
   - Provide contrasting scenarios (e.g., "When X is high, the effect is amplified because... Conversely, when X is low...").

### Step 3: Articulate the Theoretical Contribution

**Contribution Types:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution** (most critical for theory papers)
   - Extends existing theory
   - Introduces new constructs
   - Clarifies theoretical relationships
   - Resolves theoretical contradictions

2. **Practical Contribution**
   - How managers can apply the theory
   - Implications for organizational practices

3. **Methodological Contribution** (if applicable)
   - New approach to theory building

4. **Societal Contribution** (if applicable)
   - Broader implications for society

## Writing Guidelines

A well-crafted hypothesis or proposition development section usually follows a standardized **3-Paragraph Blueprint**:

### Paragraph 1: The Setup & Alignment
- **Goal:** State the expected relationship and connect it to the overarching theory.
- **Content:** "Drawing on [Theory X], we argue that [Construct A] influences [Construct B]..." Briefly review the scarce existing literature on this specific link to remind the reader of the gap.

### Paragraph 2: The Logical Deduction (The "Meat")
- **Goal:** Explain the *Why* and *How*.
- **Content:** Step-by-step causal reasoning. Use transitional phrases like *“Consequently,” “As a result,” “This occurs because…”*. 
- **Tip:** Anticipate alternative explanations and preemptively dismiss them using your theoretical logic. Provide a brief, vivid, and grounded example if the logic is highly abstract.

### Paragraph 3: The Synthesis & Formal Statement
- **Goal:** Summarize the argument into a single, testable statement.
- **Content:** "Therefore, we expect that..." followed by the indented, bolded formal statement.

## Example Proposition/Hypothesis Development Flow

**[Text leading up to the proposition - demonstrating Paragraph 2 & 3 logic]:**
"...According to conservation of resources theory, individuals strive to acquire and maintain valuable resources. When employees encounter [Construct A: e.g., abusive supervision], they experience a rapid depletion of emotional resources. To halt this resource loss, individuals instinctively shift toward self-protection mechanisms (Hobfoll, 1989). Specifically, they are likely to reduce discretionary efforts that consume additional energy, such as [Construct B: e.g., organizational citizenship behaviors]. Because [Construct A] creates a sustained state of resource threat, the cognitive bandwidth available for [Construct B] is severely restricted. Therefore, we propose:"

> **Proposition / Hypothesis 1:** [Construct A] is negatively associated with [Construct B].

**[Text for Moderation / Boundary Condition]:**
"...However, this resource depletion process is not uniform. Employees with high [Moderator: e.g., psychological resilience] possess a larger initial resource pool and faster resource recovery rates. When faced with [Construct A], highly resilient employees can buffer the emotional drain, thereby maintaining enough cognitive capacity to engage in [Construct B]. Conversely, employees with low resilience lack this buffer, making the depleting effect of [Construct A] far more severe. Therefore:"

> **Proposition / Hypothesis 2:** [Moderator] moderates the negative relationship between [Construct A] and [Construct B], such that the negative relationship is weaker when [Moderator] is high rather than low.

## Self-Evaluation Checklist

Before finalizing your theory paper, verify:
- [ ] Is the theoretical gap clearly identified?
- [ ] Are constructs clearly defined?
- [ ] Is the logical chain of reasoning complete?
- [ ] Are boundary conditions specified?
- [ ] Are propositions testable?
- [ ] Are theoretical contributions clearly articulated?
- [ ] Have you addressed alternative explanations?
