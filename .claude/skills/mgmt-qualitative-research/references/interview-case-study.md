# Interview Design & Case Study Research

## Interview Types

| Type | Characteristics | When to Use |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| **Structured** | Fixed questions, same for all | When comparability is key |
| **Semi-structured** | Core questions + probing | When flexibility needed |
| **Unstructured** | Open conversation | When exploring unknown territory |

## Developing an Interview Guide

1. **Opening Questions** (Ice breakers)
   - Build rapport
   - Establish context
   - "Tell me about your role..."

2. **Core Questions** (Based on research questions)
   - Clear, open-ended
   - Avoid leading questions
   - Focus on experiences and behaviors

3. **Probing Questions** (Elaboration)
   - "Can you give me an example?"
   - "What happened next?"
   - "How did you feel about that?"
   - "What did that mean to you?"

4. **Closing Questions** (Wrap-up)
   - "Is there anything else you'd like to add?"
   - "What have we not discussed that's important?"

### Best Practices
- Pilot test your interview guide
- Record interviews (with permission)
- Take field notes during and after
- Maintain neutrality
- Use active listening

## Case Study Research (Yin, 2018)

**When to Use:**
- How or why questions
- Little control over events
- Focus on contemporary phenomenon

**Design Components:**
1. **Case Study Questions**: What you want to learn
2. **Propositions**: Derived from theory (if available)
3. **Unit of Analysis**: What defines a case
4. **Logic Linking Data to Propositions**: How evidence connects
5. **Criteria for Interpreting Findings**: Standards for evaluation

**Case Study Types:**
- **Single Case**: When one case is sufficient
- **Multiple Cases**: When replication logic is needed
- **Embedded**: Multiple units within a single case

### Data Collection Triangulation
Use multiple sources:
- **Interviews**: Multiple perspectives
- **Observations**: Direct witnessing
- **Documents**: Archival records
- **Artifacts**: Physical evidence
