# Classic Grounded Theory: Detailed Phases

Based on Flenady et al. (2025), classic grounded theory follows these phases:

## Phase 1: Problem Identification & Theoretical Sensitivity
- Identify a substantive area of interest
- Conduct initial literature review (focus on methods, not theory)
- Develop theoretical sensitivity through:
  - Professional experience
  - Personal experience
  - Technical literature
- **Do NOT** use a full literature review to avoid bias

## Phase 2: Data Collection

**Data Sources:**
- **Interviews**: Semi-structured, open-ended, 30-90 minutes
- **Observations**: Field notes, participant observation
- **Documents**: Organizational records, emails, meeting minutes
- **Secondary data**: Publicly available data, social media

**Sampling Strategies:**
- **Theoretical Sampling**: Select participants who can help develop theory
- **Purposeful Sampling**: Select information-rich cases
- **Snowball Sampling**: Participants refer other participants

## Phase 3: Coding

**Three Types of Coding:**

1. **Open Coding** (Initial breakdown)
   - Break down data line-by-line or paragraph-by-paragraph
   - Create codes that capture the essence
   - Use gerunds (e.g., "seeking support", "negotiating meaning")
   - Stay close to the data, avoid interpretation

2. **Axial Coding** (Making connections)
   - Identify relationships between codes
   - Group codes into categories
   - Identify the core phenomenon
   - Connect conditions, actions, strategies, and consequences

3. **Selective Coding** (Integration)
   - Identify the core category
   - Integrate all categories around the core
   - Refine and develop the story line
   - Delimit the theory

**Coding Example:**

```
Original Text: "I didn't know what to do when my boss assigned me that project.
I had to figure it out on my own, asking colleagues for help and learning by doing."

Open Codes: - Uncertainty about task
           - Self-directed learning
           - Seeking peer support

Axial Category: Sense-making strategies

Core Category: Navigating ambiguity in new roles
```

## Phase 4: Constant Comparison
- Compare each incident with previous incidents
- Compare codes with other codes
- Compare categories with other categories
- Ensure data saturation (no new properties emerge)

## Phase 5: Memoing
- Write memos throughout the process
- Record ideas, thoughts, and theoretical insights
- Types of memos:
  - Code notes (about specific codes)
  - Theoretical notes (about relationships)
  - Operational notes (about methods)
  - Methodological notes (about process)

## Phase 6: Theoretical Sampling
- Sample based on emerging theory
- Seek participants who can elaborate or challenge categories
- Stop when theoretical saturation is reached

## Phase 7: Theory Integration
- Write the story of the process
- Create a visual model or diagram
- Delimit the theory (identify boundaries)
- Write the final narrative
